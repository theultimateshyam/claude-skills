# LinkedIn Posting Skill

## Purpose
Generate LinkedIn posts that reflect authentic, thoughtful observation while building genuine professional connections. This skill helps craft content that stands out through conversational voice, cross-domain thinking, and vulnerability—not through performative intellectualism or viral chasing.

## Core Voice & Philosophy

### Voice Characteristics
**Conversational intimacy**: Write as if having coffee with a curious friend—someone who values honest reflection over polished performance. Your reader should feel like they're in on an observation, not being lectured to.

**Genuine noticing**: Share what you actually observe, not what you think will perform well. The power comes from authentic attention to patterns others might have missed.

**Cross-domain fluency**: Draw connections between nature, tech, psychology, gaming, culture, and product management organically. These aren't forced analogies—they're how you naturally see the world.

**First-person reflection**: Use "I noticed," "I've been thinking," "something struck me." Own your perspective without disclaiming it into oblivion. Be vulnerable without being self-deprecating.

**Authenticity over virality**: Write for understanding and connection, not engagement metrics. If it goes viral, great. If it resonates with three people who needed it, better.

### Key Themes to Explore
- **Systems thinking**: Flywheels, buffers, power creep, feedback loops, non-linear progression, emergence
- **Mundane moments**: Sacred spaces in everyday life, meaningful clutter, nature's quiet signals
- **Cross-domain translation**: How ideas travel through time, space, and fields; pattern recognition across contexts
- **Product management lens**: Subtle observations grounded in real examples, accessible without jargon
- **Personal growth**: Reflection and experimentation over optimization and productivity porn

## LinkedIn 2025 Best Practices

### Algorithm & Engagement Fundamentals
**Dwell time is king**: The algorithm prioritizes how long people spend with your post. Dense, thoughtful content that rewards reading beats quick-scroll material.

**Comments > everything else**: Posts that spark conversation in comments get exponentially more reach. Design for dialogue, not passive consumption.

**Native content wins**: Keep links out of the main post. LinkedIn suppresses external links by up to 80%. If you must share a link, put it in the first comment.

**First hour matters**: The algorithm tests your post on a small audience first. Early engagement determines whether it gets broader distribution.

**Consistency over frequency**: Better to post 2-3 thoughtful pieces weekly than daily mediocrity. Your quality bar matters more than your posting schedule.

### Format & Structure
**Hook in first 140 characters**: LinkedIn truncates after this. Your opening must create a curiosity gap or present an observation worth clicking "see more."

**Natural formatting**: Use line breaks for readability, but avoid over-formatting. Minimal bold, no excessive emojis, headers only when truly needed. Let the content breathe.

**Length flexibility**: No prescribed word count. Some thoughts need 150 words, others need 800. Trust your instinct for what the idea demands. The algorithm doesn't penalize length—it penalizes boring.

**Visual content (when it fits)**: Images at 1200x628 pixels, but only include visuals that genuinely add to the story. No stock photos just for the sake of having an image.

**Carousels for depth**: Use carousel posts for complex ideas that benefit from structured progression, but not as a crutch for list-ification.

## Post Structures That Work

### The Observation Post
Start with something you noticed. Unpack why it caught your attention. Connect it to a broader pattern. End with an open question that invites others to share their experience.

**Example flow**:
- Opening: Specific moment or observation
- Middle: Why this matters, what pattern it reveals
- Close: "Have you noticed this?" or "What am I missing?"

### The Pattern Recognition Post
Present two seemingly unrelated domains. Show the surprising connection. Explore implications. Trust readers to follow your thinking.

**Example flow**:
- Domain A observation
- Domain B observation  
- The unexpected bridge between them
- What this reveals about both

### The Reflection Post
Share something you got wrong, learned slowly, or are still figuring out. Be specific about the journey, not prescriptive about the destination.

**Example flow**:
- What I used to think
- The moment that shifted my perspective
- What I'm learning now
- What remains uncertain

### The Question Post
Lead with genuine curiosity about something you're wrestling with. Provide enough context for others to engage meaningfully. Don't hide your own preliminary thinking—it gives people permission to explore.

**Example flow**:
- The question you're sitting with
- Why it's not obvious
- What you've considered so far
- Invitation to think alongside you

## What to Avoid (Critical)

### Content Anti-Patterns
**No listicles or frameworks**: Avoid "5 ways to..." or "The 3-step process for..." structures. If you have multiple points, weave them into prose.

**No corporate jargon**: Cut "synergy," "leverage," "deep dive," "circle back," "move the needle." Write in plain language.

**No preachy self-help**: Resist the urge to prescribe. Replace "You should" with "I've found" or "What if we tried."

**No over-explanation**: Trust your readers' intelligence. Don't hand-hold through every logical step. Leave space for them to make connections.

**No clickbait urgency**: Avoid manufactured importance or false scarcity. "This changed everything" rarely did.

**No forced connections**: If two ideas don't naturally relate, don't force them into proximity just to seem clever.

**No academic distance**: Avoid third-person observations or detached analysis. Stay in first person and stay close to experience.

**No productivity porn**: Resist optimization obsession. Not everything is a hack or a system to be gamed.

**No sanitized failure**: When sharing what went wrong, don't wrap it in a neat lesson. Some failures are messy and remain unresolved.

**No performative vulnerability**: Share what genuinely matters to understanding, not trauma for engagement. Test: would you share this over coffee with a respected colleague?

**No borrowed wisdom without synthesis**: If referencing others' ideas, show your own thinking on top. Pure curation isn't your voice.

**No trend-chasing**: Don't reference current events or hot topics just for relevance. Only engage if you have genuine perspective to add.

### Format Anti-Patterns
**No emoji overload**: One or two emojis max, and only if they genuinely add meaning. Never use them as section dividers or decoration.

**No gratuitous bold**: Bold text should emphasize genuinely critical points, not every other sentence. Most posts need zero bold text.

**No hashtag spam**: 2-3 relevant hashtags max, placed naturally in the post body or at the end. Avoid #MondayMotivation #ThoughtLeadership #Innovation spam.

**No paragraph walls**: Use line breaks to create breathing room, but don't create a new paragraph for every sentence. Find natural rhythm.

**No ALL CAPS**: Even for emphasis. It reads as shouting and undermines your credibility.

**No excessive punctuation**: One period ends a sentence. One question mark asks a question. You don't need three of each for emphasis!!!

### Voice Anti-Patterns
**No humblebragging**: Don't disguise boasts as self-deprecation. "Wow, can't believe my little startup just hit $10M ARR!" isn't humble.

**No false questions**: Don't ask rhetorical questions where you're clearly about to give "the answer." Genuine questions invite genuine dialogue.

**No guru positioning**: Avoid "I've learned in my 15 years of..." positioning unless it's genuinely relevant context. Let your thinking speak for itself.

**No vague universals**: "Everyone knows that..." or "We all struggle with..." assumptions exclude people and shut down dialogue.

**No apologizing for posting**: Never start with "Sorry for the long post" or "Hot take incoming." Own your perspective or don't post.

**No self-important urgency**: "This is the most important thing you'll read today" probably isn't. Let readers decide what matters.

## Quality Checks Before Posting

**Authenticity check**: Would you say this to a colleague you respect? Does it sound like you?

**Clarity check**: Can someone skim this in 30 seconds and grasp the core idea? Can someone read deeply and find additional layers?

**Dialogue check**: Does this invite response? Is there room for others' experience?

**Jargon check**: Would someone outside your specific domain understand this?

**Value check**: Does this give someone something to think about, or is it just filling space?

**Vulnerability check**: Are you showing real thinking, or performing a persona?

**Grammar and vocabulary check**: 
- Read aloud: Does it flow naturally? Awkward phrasing reveals itself when spoken.
- Simple > fancy: Choose "use" over "utilize," "help" over "facilitate," "about" over "regarding." Sophisticated thinking doesn't require sophisticated words.
- Active voice default: "I noticed" beats "It was noticed." Passive voice distances you from your observations.
- Punctuation rhythm: Commas create breathing room. Periods create emphasis. Em dashes—when used sparingly—add conversational asides. Don't overuse any of them.
- Consistency: If you write "it's" in one place, don't switch to "it is" elsewhere without reason. Pick your contractions and stick with them.
- Spell check the basics: Typos in "their/they're/there" or "your/you're" undermine credibility. LinkedIn doesn't forgive these.
- Vocabulary precision: Use specific words when they matter ("observe" vs. "notice" vs. "spot"), but default to simple language. A thesaurus is usually wrong.
- Avoid qualifier bloat: Cut "really," "very," "quite," "just," "actually" unless they're doing real work. "I'm curious" is stronger than "I'm actually quite curious."
- Trust the period: You don't need to end every sentence with an exclamation point! Save them for genuine excitement or surprise.

**The read-aloud test**: If you stumble reading your own post aloud, readers will stumble reading it silently. Fix the rhythm before posting.

## Engagement Strategy

### Responding to Comments
Engage meaningfully within the first hour. Don't just "thanks for sharing"—add to the conversation. Ask follow-up questions. Show genuine curiosity about others' perspectives.

### Starting Conversations
@ mention people when their expertise or experience is genuinely relevant, but do it sparingly. It should feel like naturally including someone in a conversation, not fishing for engagement.

### Building Dialogue
Some of your best content will emerge from comment threads. Pay attention to what resonates, what questions come up repeatedly, what sparks unexpected discussion. These are signals for future posts.

## Advanced Considerations

### When to Break Format
If a thought demands bullet points or a numbered list because that's genuinely the clearest structure—use it. The guideline against lists is about avoiding lazy thinking, not banning structure when it serves clarity.

### When to Add Visuals
Include images when they genuinely enhance understanding—a diagram of a system you're describing, a photo of the specific moment you're referencing. Skip the generic "person staring thoughtfully at laptop" stock photo.

### When to Go Long
If you're unpacking something complex that requires nuance and can't be reduced without losing truth—go long. Readers who care will stay with you. Those who don't weren't your audience anyway.

### When to Stay Silent
Not everything needs to be a post. If you're still figuring something out and can't articulate it without feeling performative—wait. Better silence than noise.

### When to Reference Newsletter/Blog
Include references to ultimatetale.substack.com (newsletter) or ultimateblog.vercel.app (blog) when:

**The post is adapted from that content**: "I explored this in my newsletter" signals where the thinking originated and invites deeper reading.

**You've written technical depth that doesn't fit LinkedIn**: Blog posts can go deeper into implementation details, code examples, or system architecture that would overwhelm a LinkedIn post.

**The newsletter expands on a pattern**: If your LinkedIn post introduces an observation and your newsletter explores variations, edge cases, or related patterns—mention it.

**Natural curiosity point**: If someone reading your post would organically wonder "but how does this apply to X?"—and you've answered that on your blog—share it.

**Don't reference when**:
- You're just trying to drive traffic (readers can smell this)
- The LinkedIn post is already complete (no need to fragment attention)
- You're referencing "my latest post" without specific value-add
- It would break the conversational flow

**How to word it naturally**:
- "I've been thinking about this in my newsletter—specifically how..."
- "Wrote about the technical implementation on my blog if you're curious about..."
- "This connects to something I explored in my newsletter about..."
- "For the folks who want to go deeper on the systems side, I broke it down on my blog"

**Where to put the link**:
Primary mention in post body (brief, conversational), then full URL in first comment. This keeps the post clean while giving the algorithm-friendly signal of native content.

**Test**: Would you mention this to a colleague in conversation? "Oh yeah, I wrote about that in my newsletter" is natural. "Check out my newsletter for more content!" is not.

## Success Metrics (Use Sparingly)

Track these loosely to calibrate, but never write to them:
- Comments vs. likes ratio (higher is better—means you sparked conversation)
- Depth of comment threads (genuine dialogue vs. drive-by reactions)
- Follow-up questions in DMs (signal you're making people think)
- Quality of who engages (respected voices in your space)

Ignore these entirely:
- Total likes
- Follower count growth rate
- Views on individual posts
- Comparison to others' performance

## Final Reminders

Your voice is your unfair advantage. Everyone has access to the same LinkedIn algorithm, the same posting tips, the same best practices. What they don't have is your specific combination of experiences, observations, and ways of seeing patterns.

Write from that place. The authentic, specific, vulnerable, curious place where you're genuinely trying to figure something out and share what you're noticing.

That's what cuts through the noise. Not because it's optimized for engagement, but because it's true and therefore rare.
